<section className="mb-14">
  <h2 className="text-display-md text-white text-balance mb-5">
    The schema bundle.
  </h2>
  <p className="text-body-md text-silver mb-8">
    Five Schema.org types do the heavy lifting at Stage 3. The engines lean on JSON-LD; alternative encodings (microdata, RDFa) work in principle but JSON-LD is the format every major engine documents first and supports most reliably. Inline the schemas in the page&apos;s rendered HTML — not in a hydration payload a non-JS crawler will miss.
  </p>

    <div className="rounded-[var(--radius-lg)] border border-[var(--color-border-default)] bg-[var(--color-surface-elevated)] p-6 md:p-7">

        <p className="text-body-xs font-mono text-electric">Article</p>
        <h3 className="text-heading-md text-white">The wrapper</h3>
      </div>
      <p className="text-body-md text-silver">
        Every methodology piece, brief, case study, or thought-leadership page should wrap in an Article schema. The minimum fields are headline, description, datePublished, dateModified, author, and image. Article is the surface where founder voice (Stage 5 of the hierarchy) gets attributed; engines that resolve a credible author tend to up-weight the cited passage. Article also tells the engine the page is editorial, not promotional — which matters when the engine is choosing between a vendor blog and an analyst report.
      </p>
    </div>
    <div className="rounded-[var(--radius-lg)] border border-[var(--color-border-default)] bg-[var(--color-surface-elevated)] p-6 md:p-7">

        <p className="text-body-xs font-mono text-electric">FAQPage</p>
        <h3 className="text-heading-md text-white">The answer harvester</h3>
      </div>
      <p className="text-body-md text-silver">
        FAQPage is the most under-shipped Stage 3 lever in the agency category. When a page genuinely answers eight to ten buyer-decision questions, FAQPage schema hands the engine a pre-extracted Q&amp;A block ready to quote verbatim. The two anti-patterns to avoid are using FAQPage for marketing fluff (engines penalise promotional FAQ) and duplicating the on-page FAQ inside the schema instead of mirroring it (engines flag the mismatch). Match the schema content to the visible content exactly, and reserve FAQ for genuine buyer questions, not objection-handling.
      </p>
    </div>

      <p className="text-body-md text-silver">
        HowTo applies when a page documents a procedure — a checklist, a sequence of steps, a methodology in stages. The Five-Stage Citation Hierarchy itself is the kind of content HowTo was designed for. Each step gets a name, text, and optionally an image. Engines reward HowTo because the structure maps cleanly to procedural prompts, the ones starting with &quot;how do I&quot; or &quot;what&apos;s the process for.&quot; Apply HowTo on methodology pages, audit-roadmap pages, and any page that walks a buyer through a sequence.

Critical detail: the Person.sameAs must point to the human&apos;s personal profiles, not the company&apos;s. Pointing Person.sameAs at the company LinkedIn collapses the human and the brand into one entity, dilutes citation attribution, and undercuts the founder-voice signal — see <Link href="/labs/five-citation-signals" className="text-electric hover:text-electric-light underline-offset-4 hover:underline">the five signals breakdown</Link> for the wider context.

It tells the engine where the page sits in the site hierarchy, which lets the engine reason about authority transfer from the parent surface (a /citation-brief index page lifts each individual brief; a /systems/ai-visibility lifts each child page). BreadcrumbList is also the schema Google&apos;s SERP enrichment leans on for the breadcrumb display under blue links. Cheap to ship, runs on every page, no excuse to skip it.

<section className="mb-14">
  <h2 className="text-display-md text-white text-balance mb-5">
    The extractability content pattern.
  </h2>
  <p className="text-body-md text-silver mb-4">
    Schema is necessary but not sufficient. The content itself has to be shaped for extraction. Five elements compose the Wiele extractability pattern, and they reinforce one another — the bundle works because every element nudges the engine toward the same conclusion: this page contains a clean, attributable answer to the prompt.
  </p>
  <ol className="space-y-4 text-body-md text-silver list-decimal pl-6">
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">The definitive opener.</strong> The first 100 to 200 words must contain a quotable answer to the page&apos;s implied question. Not a tease, not a setup, not a marketing hook — the answer itself, written in the third person, framed as if it could be lifted verbatim into an AI response. The 60-second-answer block at the top of every Wiele Citation Brief is the canonical implementation. Engines that scan a page in milliseconds will pull the first extractable block they find; if your first block is &quot;we believe&quot; and a competitor&apos;s is &quot;the five signals are…&quot;, the engine picks the competitor.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">A predictable H2 / H3 hierarchy.</strong> Two levels deep, semantic headings, no skipped levels. Every H2 names a substantive section; every H3 names a substantive subsection. Engines map the heading tree onto a passage-retrieval index — the cleaner the tree, the more accurately the engine locates the right passage to cite. Marketing-style headings (questions framed for click-bait, all-caps slogans) confuse the retrieval; declarative headings outperform them across every engine class.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">Table-or-list layouts where the data permits.</strong> Pricing comparisons, feature breakdowns, multi-step procedures, multi-option trade-offs — anything that benefits from rows-and-columns or numbered-list structure should ship as a table or list, not as prose. Engines extract tables and lists almost atomically; prose containing the same data extracts less reliably. The cost is the discipline of structuring information; the reward is preferential citation.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">Internal anchor links.</strong> Every H2 should resolve to a stable URL fragment (e.g. <code>/citation-brief/stage-3-structured-extractability#the-schema-bundle</code>). Anchors let the engine cite a sub-section URL rather than the whole page — finer-grained attribution that survives content updates and lifts citation precision. Cheap to implement; Wiele&apos;s mdx-components automatically wires this via rehype-slug.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">On-page citations and source attribution.</strong> When the page asserts a fact, it names the source — Schema.org spec, Google Search Central documentation, public engine guidance, or the brand&apos;s own primary research. On-page citations signal to the engine that the page itself participates in the citation graph, which compounds with Stage 2 (Source Authority) and Stage 5 (Recommendation History) over time.
    </li>
  </ol>
</section>

<section className="mb-14">
  <h2 className="text-display-md text-white text-balance mb-5">
    What NOT to do.
  </h2>
  <p className="text-body-md text-silver mb-4">
    Five recurring Stage 3 antipatterns Wiele sees across pre-engagement audits — each one is a quiet citation-share leak.
  </p>
  <ul className="space-y-3 text-body-md text-silver list-disc pl-6">
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">Burying the answer three scrolls deep.</strong> The page eventually answers the question, but only after a 600-word setup, a brand origin story, and a manifesto on AI search. Engines that pull the first extractable block from the first 200 words never see the actual answer. Lead with it; expand around it.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">Schema injected via client-side JavaScript.</strong> Some implementations inject JSON-LD via a runtime script tag after hydration. Non-JS crawlers (including the AI-engine crawlers that prioritise speed over execution) miss the schema entirely. Inline the JSON-LD in the rendered HTML response — server-side or static-rendered, not hydrated.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">FAQPage stuffed with marketing FAQ.</strong> Schema.org defines FAQPage for genuine question-and-answer content. Pages that wrap promotional CTAs (&quot;Why choose us?&quot; &quot;How long until I see results?&quot;) in FAQPage schema get flagged by Google&apos;s rich-result eligibility filter and silently dropped from the answer set. Reserve FAQPage for buyer-decision questions with substantive answers.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">H1 + paragraph-only structure.</strong> A 3,000-word page with a single H1 and no H2 / H3 breakdown is a wall of prose with no extraction handles. The engine can&apos;t locate the right passage; the founder voice is uniform but un-quotable. Two levels of semantic heading hierarchy, minimum, for any page over 800 words.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong className="text-white">No author attribution.</strong> The page is unsigned, no Person schema, no byline, no bio link. Founder voice (Stage 5 of the hierarchy) evaporates — the engine can&apos;t weigh the author&apos;s authority because the author doesn&apos;t exist as an entity. Name the author; ship Person schema with the correct sameAs; link to a bio that the engine can crawl.
    </li>
  </ul>
</section>
